Make Gofu

A simple, adaptable bean-based method. Start small. Adjust as you go.

Before you begin

Gofu is a prototype food: a living recipe meant to be adapted. The steps below offer one way to turn dry beans + water into a tofu-like base. Different beans will behave differently, and that’s part of the design.

Start with small batches. Take notes. Share what you learn.

Always follow basic food safety: clean hands and tools, fresh water, proper soaking/cooking of beans, and sensible storage (refrigerate and use within a few days).

Base method (v1)

This is a simple starting point. Adjust ratios and timing based on your beans, your stove, and your taste.

Ingredients (small batch)

  • Dry beans: about 1 cup (e.g. white beans, chickpeas, or another mild bean)
  • Water: enough to soak + later blend (see below)
  • Salt: a pinch or two
  • Coagulant / setting helper (optional, experimental): a splash of acid (lemon juice or vinegar) or a small amount of salt-only brine

Equipment

  • Pot for cooking beans
  • Bowl for soaking
  • Blender or sturdy hand blender
  • Fine strainer, nut-milk bag, or clean cloth
  • Heat-safe container or shallow dish for setting the Gofu

Steps

  1. Soak the beans.
    Rinse 1 cup of dry beans. Soak in plenty of water for several hours or overnight, until the beans are plump. Drain and rinse.
  2. Cook until very soft.
    Cover the soaked beans with fresh water and cook until they are soft enough to easily mash between your fingers. This may take 45–90 minutes depending on the bean. Lightly salt toward the end of cooking.
  3. Blend with fresh water.
    Drain the cooked beans (saving a little cooking liquid if you like). Blend the beans with enough fresh water to make a smooth, pourable mixture — thicker than plant milk, thinner than hummus. Aim for a texture like heavy cream or thin batter.
  4. Heat the bean cream.
    Pour the blended mixture into a pot. Gently heat on medium-low, stirring often to prevent sticking or scorching. You want it hot and gently simmering, not rapidly boiling.
  5. Encourage it to set.
    This is where experimentation lives. You can:
    • continue cooking and stirring until it thickens on its own, then
    • or add a small splash of lemon juice or vinegar, stirring gently,
    • or pour into a container and let it cool and firm in the fridge.
    Different beans will thicken in different ways.
  6. Cool and shape.
    Pour the thickened mixture into a lightly oiled or lined container. Let it cool, then refrigerate until firm enough to slice or crumble.

You now have a basic Gofu: soft, sliceable, and ready to season, marinate, fry, crumble into salads, or fold into stews.

Adjustments & variations

Texture

  • Softer Gofu: add more water when blending.
  • Firmer Gofu: use less water, cook longer, and chill thoroughly.
  • Creamy spread: stop before it fully sets and use as a warm dip or chilled spread.

Flavor

  • Add garlic, herbs, or spices during the final heating step.
  • Marinate cooled slices in oil, vinegar, and herbs.
  • Crumble and sauté with onions and spices for a “scramble.”

Beans

  • Try different beans separately and note how they behave.
  • Record which beans give the best texture for your climate and kitchen.
  • Share your findings with others so the commons can grow.

There is no single “correct” Gofu. The goal is a family of related foods that honor local beans, local water, and local tastes.

Safety & storage

Gofu is a fresh food. Treat it like cooked beans or homemade tofu:

  • Refrigerate promptly after it cools.
  • Use clean utensils when handling.
  • Enjoy within 3–4 days for best quality.
  • If it smells off or looks questionable, don’t eat it.

This page is a starting pattern, not medical or nutritional advice. Always adapt to your own health needs and local food safety guidance.
