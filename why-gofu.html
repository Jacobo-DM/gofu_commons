Why Gofu?

The case for a more adaptable, local, commons-based food technology.

Across the world, food systems are under growing strain. Climate disruption, fertilizer shortages, fragile supply chains, and the risks of monoculture farming are becoming harder to ignore. Many communities are asking the same question:

How do we create food systems that are more flexible, resilient, and local?

Gofu is a small answer to that question.

Beyond Soy

Traditional tofu is made from soybeans. It is a remarkable food, but relying on a single crop has limits.

Gofu is a simple idea: tofu-style protein can be made from many legumes, seeds, and regional plants.

Chickpeas, lentils, peas, fava beans, and other crops can be used to create nourishing, tofu-like foods using accessible kitchen methods.

This opens the door for communities around the world to adapt the method using the crops that grow best where they live.

Simple and Adaptable

Gofu is intentionally low-tech.

It can be made with basic kitchen tools and simple ingredients. The goal is not perfection — it is adaptability.

  • Use what grows well locally.
  • Experiment with flavor and texture.
  • Share what works.
  • Let communities shape their own variations.

In this way, Gofu is less a single recipe than a shared method.

Fresh and Fermented Paths

Gofu can be made fresh and used immediately, but fermentation also opens interesting possibilities.

Fermentation may improve flavor, preservation, and digestibility while allowing local food traditions to influence the process.

Because fermentation involves living cultures, experiments should be done carefully with attention to cleanliness and food safety.

Over time, communities may develop their own fermented gofu traditions just as cultures around the world have developed fermented foods from beans, grains, and vegetables.

A Commons Food

Gofu is shared as a commons idea.

It is not a product and not a brand. It is an invitation to experiment, adapt, and share knowledge so that more people can access simple sources of nourishment.

If food systems are going to become more resilient, many small ideas like this will need to spread, evolve, and take root in different places.

Gofu is one small seed in that direction.
