Why Gofu?

A little food. A bigger intention.

From our kitchen — a short origin

Gofu began when I wanted to make a healthier version of my mom’s beloved chicken salad. I thought of tofu — but I hesitated, aware of how much of the global tofu supply depends on soybeans.

“What if we swap out that bean for a different one — something gentler, more flexible, more local — and turn beans + water into fresh, nourishing protein?”

I started mixing other beans, water, simple tools we already had in the kitchen. Little experiments. Small meals. And eventually — Gofu was born. A humble bean-based food, but one rooted in the warmth of a family kitchen and a wish for something that felt right in body and place.

The broader context: why another bean — not soy

Globally, soybean farming — especially for commodity crops, animal feed, processed foods — has significant environmental and social impacts. Evidence shows that millions of hectares of forests and native ecosystems have been cleared to make way for soy cultivation. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

For example, between 2001 and 2015, about 8.2 million hectares of land were replaced by soy cultivation — and over 97% of that occurred in South America. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1} Forests, savannahs, biodiverse habitats like the Amazon rainforest, the Cerrado and other native ecosystems — are threatened when land is cleared for soy, then monocropped. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

The consequences aren’t only ecological. Soil degradation, erosion, fertilizer and pesticide runoff, loss of biodiversity, and disruption of local communities have been documented in regions dominated by industrial-scale soy farming. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5} In short: a food system built on soy monocultures often carries hidden costs far beyond the soybeans in the grocery aisle.

Gofu as a regenerative evolution of tofu-tech

Gofu isn’t just a taste preference or dietary choice — it’s a small act of resistance and regeneration. By re-imagining “tofu” away from its soybean roots, we open the door to practices that:

  • celebrate bean diversity — beans native or adapted to local soils and climates;
  • reduce reliance on large monocultures that drive deforestation and biodiversity loss;
  • promote food sovereignty and accessibility — by using affordable, widely available beans;
  • invite kitchens — home, community, school — to participate in a shared food-making tradition that can be adapted, improved, and passed on.

In a world increasingly in need of regenerative practices — practices that honor the needs of ecosystems and the beings who depend on them — Gofu is a gentle seed. A seed for reconnecting food with place, for nourishing people without starving the land.

Invite to adapt, share & regenerate

This work is offered in the spirit of generosity and collaboration. Everyone is invited to try Gofu, adapt it to their beans and tastes, share their own variations — and contribute to a growing commons of recipes, stories, and sustainable food practice.

If you do try it: consider sharing what bean you used, where you live, why you made it. Over time, Gofu can grow not just as a recipe — but as a living network: of kitchens, of soils, of hope.
