Why Gofu?

A little food. A bigger intention.

From our kitchen — a short origin

My mom loves chicken salad. I wanted to make a healthier version for her, so I thought of tofu as a substitute. Then I paused, remembering how much of the global tofu supply depends on soybeans and on large industrial systems I didn’t fully trust.

I wondered: “What if we swap out that bean for a different one — something gentler, more flexible, more local — and still turn beans + water into fresh, nourishing protein?”

So I started experimenting with other beans: water, heat, salt, and simple tools already in the kitchen. Little tests. Small meals. Some failures, some surprises — and slowly, Gofu appeared:

a humble bean–based food, rooted in the warmth of a family kitchen and in the wish for something that felt right in both body and place.

The broader context: why another bean — not soy

Soybeans themselves are not the “villain” — they’re an important crop in many cultures. The concern is more about how soy is grown and used at industrial scale: huge monocultures, long-distance shipping, and heavy dependence on chemical inputs.

In many regions, forests, savannahs, and native ecosystems have been cleared to make room for soy fields. Monoculture farming often brings soil degradation, erosion, fertilizer and pesticide runoff, loss of biodiversity, and pressure on local communities.

When a single global crop sits at the center of animal feed, processed foods, and countless consumer products, the hidden costs add up — even if it looks harmless in the grocery aisle.

Gofu doesn’t exist against tofu or soy, but as a small experiment in another direction: one that asks, “What happens when we shift the bean, and bring the technique closer to local soils, local kitchens, and local needs?”

Gofu as a regenerative evolution of tofu-tech

Gofu is not just a taste preference or diet tweak. It’s a small act of resistance and regeneration: keeping the elegant logic of tofu-tech (beans + water → fresh protein), but inviting different beans, different stories, and different places into the process.

In practice, that means Gofu aims to:

  • Celebrate bean diversity — using beans that are native or adapted to local soils and climates.
  • Reduce reliance on large monocultures that drive deforestation and biodiversity loss.
  • Support food sovereignty and accessibility by using affordable, widely available beans that communities can source for themselves.
  • Invite shared practice — home kitchens, community kitchens, school kitchens — to participate in a food-making tradition that can be adapted, improved, and passed on.

In a world that urgently needs more regenerative practices — ones that honor ecosystems and the people who depend on them — Gofu is meant to be a gentle seed: a way to reconnect food with place, and nourishment with respect for the land.

Invite to adapt, share & regenerate

This work is offered in the spirit of generosity and collaboration. Everyone is invited to try Gofu, adapt it to their beans and tastes, and share their own variations. Over time, the recipe is less important than the living network that grows around it: kitchens, soils, stories, and care.

If you do try it, consider sharing:

  • which bean you used,
  • where you live,
  • and why you made it.

This is how Gofu can travel — not as a product to be controlled, but as a commons practice that many hands can carry, repair, and reshape.
