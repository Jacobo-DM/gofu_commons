Gofu Recipes

These are starting points. Adjust freely for your beans, your water, your people.

1. Base Gofu

Ingredients (example batch)

  • 1 kg dried beans (any kind you trust and love)
  • Water for soaking + cooking
  • Salt to taste
  • Optional: garlic, onion, herbs, spices, oil or fat available

Method (simple version)

  • Rinse the beans. Soak in plenty of water until they swell and soften (overnight is ideal; shorter soaks work with hot water).
  • Drain, rinse, and cook the beans in fresh water until completely soft.
  • Blend, mash, or paddle the cooked beans with enough cooking liquid to make a smooth, thick, pourable base — like a cross between batter and porridge.
  • Season with salt and any aromatics you like. This is base Gofu: it can be ladled into bowls, poured into pans, or cooled and sliced.

From here, you can fry it, bake it, thin it into soup, or chill it to slice.

2. Family Gofu

Once the base is made, every household can adjust it to taste and budget. A few examples:

  • Add sautéed onions, garlic, and spices for a richer flavor.
  • Stir in leftover rice or grains to stretch the batch.
  • Fold in chopped greens at the end of cooking.
  • Top with anything: chili oil, herbs, grated vegetables, pickles.

3. Kudzu Gofu (or Thickening Root Version)

In regions where kudzu or similar starches are available, a small amount can be used to give Gofu extra body and bounce.

  • Prepare base Gofu.
  • In a bowl, dissolve 1–2 tablespoons of kudzu (or other starch) in cold water until smooth.
  • Stir this slurry into hot Gofu while mixing constantly.
  • Simmer gently for a few minutes until thicker and glossy.

This version can set more firmly when cooled, useful for slicing and frying.

4. School & Large-Kitchen Gofu

For cafeterias, shelters, and community kitchens serving many people, the same pattern scales up:

  • Soak beans in large tubs or cambros; cook in steam kettles or large pots.
  • Use immersion blenders or paddles to transform cooked beans to Gofu.
  • Season simply so students and guests can add toppings as they wish.
  • Serve with bread, rice, tortillas, or vegetables — whatever is local and loved.

Staff and students can co-design toppings and side dishes; Gofu becomes a base for creativity, not a fixed product.

These recipes are starting points. Please adapt them to your land, your budget, and your people — and share back what you discover.
